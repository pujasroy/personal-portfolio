Lava of Hate

| prose

There was a time when we were good—close friends.

I trusted you as one of my confidants and crowned you with the title of magnanimity.

Perhaps it was too grand a name, bestowed far too soon.


Had I waited longer, I might have tasted the venom beneath your sweetness—

jealousy simmering quietly, layered under honeyed words and tender gestures.

Your true feelings were never meant to be known,

hidden behind flattery and practiced warmth.

And so, I was deceived—oblivious to the intentions you harbored within.


I believed that staying with someone long enough binds you to them,

that attachment grows and you begin to reshape yourself for their sake.

I was wrong.


Within a month, fate acquainted me with a brutal truth:

I could never pretend to be a comrade to someone

who carried a sword for my back.

The precision with which you betrayed me,

wielding the authority you held, was almost admirable in its cruelty.


I may spend the rest of my life trying to heal—

convincing myself it was my fault, digesting the damage,

and learning to let go of someone who called me a friend

only when it served them.


Years have passed, and it shames me that I still haven’t moved on.

Not because you were irreplaceable,

but because I haven’t found someone worthy of the honesty I offer.

The hollow you carved into my heart may never be filled,

and perhaps I must learn to live with that absence.


I don’t wish to fill it with another person.

I refuse to use someone as a remedy for old wounds.


Because unlike you,

the blood that runs through my veins

does not yearn to erupt into a lava of hate.
